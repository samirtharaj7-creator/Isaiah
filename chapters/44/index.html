Isaiah 44: Comfort and the God Who Carries

Isaiah Chapter 44

Isaiah announces comfort, unmasks idols, and reveals the Lord as Creator, Redeemer, and the One who carries His people home.

Study Notes

Isaiah 44:1

“But now hear.” Once again after rebuke comes renewed address. Jacob is still called God’s servant, Israel still the one he has chosen. The “but now” is full of covenant mercy. The people who had burdened God with sins are not beyond his voice. He still speaks to them as his own. That is how grace starts restoring the heart. He does not begin here with another accusation, though accusation was deserved. He begins by summoning them to listen again. Hearing is the first movement back from blindness. Their identity also matters. They are Jacob and Israel—both the memory of weakness and the name of covenant calling. God does not deny either. He addresses the same people who failed and reminds them that his choosing has not disappeared. The line should steady those who imagine that repeated failure must have silenced God for good. The Lord may rebuke severely, but he is still able to say “my servant.” His commitment outlasts our confusion. Therefore the proper response is not hiding but listening. Grace speaks again. The question is whether the weary heart will finally hear.

Isaiah 44:2

The Lord identifies himself as the one who made, formed from the womb, and helps his people. Then he says, “Fear not,” and addresses them as Jeshurun, a tender covenant name. The progression is beautiful. Their history begins in God’s forming care, and their present is sustained by his help. He did more than make them and step away. He remains near as helper. The mention of the womb underscores how early and how intimate his claim is. His people are not late additions to his concern. He has known them from their beginning. Jeshurun adds affection. It evokes the people as they are meant to be under God’s favor, not just as they have behaved in their sin. That is one of grace’s lovely ways. God addresses his people not just by their failure but also by the identity his love gives them. “Fear not” therefore rests on more than comforting words. It rests on the God who formed them, still knows them, and has not withdrawn his help. The church lives from the same miracle: God keeps speaking hope into those he himself has made his own.

Isaiah 44:3

The promise of water returns, but now it is explicitly linked with the Spirit and blessing. The Lord will pour water on the thirsty land and streams on the dry ground; he will pour his Spirit on offspring and blessing on descendants. Isaiah's imagery and the reality belong together. Just as parched earth cannot revive itself, so covenant life cannot be renewed by human effort alone. God must pour. The repeated verb is crucial. Spiritual life is gift, not manufacture. The promise stretches beyond the first hearers to their children, showing that God’s restoring purpose includes future generations. He does more than rescue the present remnant and leave the next generation to fend for itself. He intends continuity of grace. The thirsty land image also reminds us how God reads his people’s need. He does not treat them as mildly undernourished. They are dry, and what they need is not advice but outpouring. The Spirit is God’s own life-giving presence, the answer to dull hearts, dead religion, and inherited weakness. Where the Spirit is poured, blessing becomes more than memory. It becomes living power.

Isaiah 44:4

The descendants will spring up among the grass like willows by flowing streams. The picture is one of visible freshness, vitality, and stability. Willows by water do more than survive; they flourish because they are set in a place of supply. That is the effect of the Spirit and blessing promised in the previous verse. God’s renewing work bears fruit in growth that is both obvious and organic. This is encouraging for all who long to see life where dryness has dominated. Renewal may seem impossible when the ground has been hard for a long time, but God’s outpouring changes the environment of the soul and the community. Growth then becomes not strained performance but the natural expression of life received. The scene of rising plants also hints at hope across generations. New life appears where barrenness seemed likely to continue. The church should take courage here. God is able to create holy vitality in places that looked spiritually exhausted. What he pours in grace grows in life. Willows by streams are not monuments to human discipline. They are witnesses to divine provision.

Isaiah 44:5

The fruit of renewal is joyful belonging. One says, “I am the LORD’s.” Another takes the name of Jacob. Another writes on his hand, “The LORD’s,” and names himself by Israel’s name. This sentence is full of glad identification. No one is being forced into cold formality. The people openly claim the Lord as their own and themselves as his. That is what the outpoured Spirit produces. It heals the heart’s reluctance and replaces it with willing confession. Writing on the hand suggests marked allegiance, a public, embodied sign of belonging. In a world of competing loyalties, this is precious. God’s redemption does more than remove guilt; it creates joyful ownership. People who once were weary of him now gladly speak his name over themselves. This sentence may also hint at the widening of the people of God, as those not formerly within Israel’s visible bounds come to identify themselves with the Lord and with his people. In any case, the center is clear: restoration leads to confession. The renewed heart does not hesitate to say, “I belong to him.” That is one of grace’s clearest fruits.

Isaiah 44:6

The Lord speaks as King of Israel, Redeemer, and Lord of hosts: “I am the first and I am the last; besides me there is no god.” These titles gather transcendence, covenant closeness, and sovereign power into one declaration. He is King, yet also Redeemer. He commands hosts, yet gives himself to Israel. “First and last” means he is source and goal, before all and beyond all. Nothing gives rise to him, and nothing outlasts him. Therefore no other god exists beside him. This is more than a statement of religious opinion. It is the deepest reality under everything. For frightened people, this is comfort. For idolaters, it is warning. For all, it is a summons to truth. The God who holds time and rules hosts is the same God who redeems his people. For that reason faith can rest. If he were only first and last but not Redeemer, the soul would remain distant. If he were Redeemer but not first and last, hope would be fragile. In him both meet. Absolute sovereignty and covenant mercy belong together. That is the heart of biblical monotheism.

Isaiah 44:7

The challenge follows naturally: who is like the Lord? Let him proclaim it, declare it, and set it in order. Let him tell what is coming and what will happen. The Lord’s uniqueness is tested in his mastery of history. He does more than know isolated facts. He orders events, appoints peoples, and gives history a coherent shape. The reference to appointing an ancient people highlights his long, public track record. Israel’s existence is evidence. Unlike idols, the Lord has acted in a sustained and historical way over generations. Therefore he can challenge any rival to match that. Here the prophet also implies that truth about the future belongs with truth about the past. Only the one who set things in motion can rightly explain what they mean and where they are going. This explains why human wisdom apart from God so often feels fragmented. It may describe details, but it cannot set the whole in order. The Lord can. He calls history into being and then interprets it. That remains the basis of prophecy, witness, and hope. God’s people are not left reading random events. They live under the word of the one who orders them.

Isaiah 44:8

“Fear not, nor be afraid.” The Lord again calms his people by reminding them of prior revelation: “Have I not told you from of old and declared it?” Then he says, “You are my witnesses.” Finally comes the question, “Is there a God besides me?” and the answer, “There is no Rock; I know not any.” The logic is pastoral. Fear is answered by remembered truth. God’s people can stand because he has already spoken. His past declarations supply present steadiness. Witness grows from memory. They are not called to invent a message, only to testify to what they have heard and seen. The title “Rock” is especially comforting. It speaks of solidity, refuge, reliability, and enduring strength. In a chapter exposing idols made from wood and metal, the Lord says there is no Rock besides him. Nothing else under heaven can bear the weight of trust. That helps explain why fear can be relinquished. If there were many contested deities, anxiety would be reasonable. But if the Lord alone is God, and if he has already declared his purposes, then trembling may give way to witness. Courage is not born from self-confidence. It is born from the reality of God.

Isaiah 44:9

All who fashion idols are nothing, and the things they delight in do not profit. Their witnesses neither see nor know. Isaiah begins the satire with stark moral and spiritual diagnosis. The makers are empty because they have turned from the living God to what cannot speak. Their cherished objects are “delights,” which hints at the emotional pull of idolatry. People do not cling to idols only by cold reasoning. They love them. So idolatry is so tenacious. It captures desire as much as thought. Yet what they love cannot profit. The word cuts sharply. Idols promise much—security, identity, control, comfort—but give no real return. Their witnesses are blind because the system itself produces blindness. Those who defend idols become like them. False worship dulls perception. The end is shame because what has been loved and defended is exposed as void. This remains a searching portrait of the human heart. Whatever we delight in apart from God, and trust to give what only he can give, becomes worthless in the moment of truth. What enchants us may still not save us.

Isaiah 44:10

The question is simple and devastating: who fashions a god or casts an idol that is profitable for nothing? Isaiah wants the absurdity to be seen plainly. A “god” made by human hands has already been disqualified by the making. The one who forms it has demonstrated its dependence before it ever exists. Isaiah's phrase “profitable for nothing” keeps returning us to the same reality: idols cannot give what they promise. In one sense, the whole long satire that follows is contained in this verse. Why would anyone invest labor, imagination, and devotion into something that cannot benefit the soul? Yet people do it constantly. That shows how deep spiritual deception runs. The heart can prefer manufactured hope over living truth. Isaiah’s question therefore is more than intellectual. It is meant to unmask desire. Why am I so willing to make ultimate what I know is finite? Why do I keep bending toward what cannot rescue? The prophet does not leave us in abstract condemnation. He presses the absurdity until we can no longer pretend our substitutes are reasonable. Sometimes freedom begins with seeing how foolish our dearest false trusts really are.

Isaiah 44:11

All the companions of the idol-maker shall be put to shame, and the craftsmen are only human. Let them all gather and stand; they shall be terrified and ashamed together. The line widens the exposure from the object to the whole network around it. The text shows makers, admirers, defenders, and fellow participants. Idolatry is often communal. Entire systems of honor and meaning grow up around what is false. Yet when brought into God’s court, the whole arrangement collapses together. The crucial phrase is “only human.” The craftsmen are not divine mediators. They are frail creatures. However skilled, they cannot produce godhood out of their work. Their humanity is not contemptible in itself; it becomes tragic when it tries to generate ultimate reality. The gathering and standing here echo courtroom language. Let the whole guild come forward. Let the supporters assemble. In the end, they will be afraid because all their confidence rested on something incapable of bearing scrutiny. The text warns against the comfort of collective self-deception. A community can reinforce a lie, but it cannot turn the lie into truth. Shared enthusiasm does not create living power.

Isaiah 44:12

The ironsmith works over the coals, shapes with hammers, and labors with the strength of his arm, yet grows hungry, thirsty, and faint. Isaiah is not condemning craftsmanship as such. He is exposing the impossibility of deriving deity from laboring humanity. The smith’s energy is limited. He gets tired. He needs food and water. The supposed god comes into being through the diminishing strength of a creature. The metaphor is quietly humiliating. A finite, weakening man is producing what others will later treat as ultimate. The idol cannot rise above its origin. This also reveals the bondage of idolatry. The maker gives himself to producing a savior, but in the process his own frailty is highlighted. He is faint while forging a false strength. That is still true of modern idols. People exhaust themselves crafting identities, systems, and securities that promise control, yet the process itself reveals their neediness. Human strength poured into self-made salvation does not create freedom. It creates further weariness. The idol begins in fatigue and ends in delusion. By contrast, the Lord gives strength to the faint; he is never fashioned by the faint.

Isaiah 44:13

Now the carpenter enters. He measures, marks, planes, and shapes the wood into the figure of a man, with the beauty of man, to dwell in a house. The satire sharpens. The idol is modeled on humanity. The highest image the craftsman can imagine is still only human form. The gods of the nations, then, reflect the imagination of their makers. They are not revelation from above but projection from below. That remains one of Scripture’s great exposures of false religion. People fashion a deity after the measure of what they already find admirable or familiar. The idol becomes a mirror with divine pretensions. The wording “to dwell in a house” adds another layer. The god needs housing. It is placed, kept, and maintained like household property. What people worship is often more domesticated than they realize. They want a deity manageable enough to fit their world and support their assumptions. Isaiah refuses that possibility. The true God made humanity; humanity does not make him. Whenever worship begins by reducing God to our preferred image, the result is not devotion but idolatry.

Here Isaiah refuses vagueness and makes the spiritual issue concrete. Idols are not harmless mistakes; they train dependence on what has no life. Isaiah wants yieldedness, not admiration from a distance.

Isaiah 44:14

The craftsman cuts cedars, chooses cypress or oak, lets them grow strong among the trees, plants a cedar, and rain nourishes it. Isaiah continues tracing the idol all the way back to its earthly beginnings. Before it is revered, it is simply wood—grown, planted, watered, and part of creation. The concern is more than that trees are ordinary. It is that what becomes an idol once depended entirely on God’s ordinary providence. Rain nourished it. Soil held it. Growth came by a creation order established by the Lord. Nothing in the material itself announces divinity. It is creaturely from first to last. This is why idolatry is so upside down. Human beings take something sustained by God and then ask it to save them from need. The thing that once needed rain is now expected to rescue souls. The same pattern repeats whenever created gifts are turned into ultimate trusts. Good things, even beautiful and useful things, are made to carry a weight they were never meant to bear. Isaiah teaches us to re-see material reality. Creation is good, but it is not God.

Isaiah 44:15

The tree becomes fuel. A man uses part of it to warm himself and bake bread. But then, from that same material, he makes a god and worships it. Isaiah’s point is not that ordinary uses are low and religious uses are high. At stake is that the same object cannot be common material in one moment and ultimate deity in the next. The problem is not bread or fire. It is the irrational leap into worship. The craftsman’s own use of the wood for daily needs proves what it is. It is a creaturely resource. Yet he then bows before the remainder as if essence had changed. This is the madness of idolatry: treating as divine what we already know to be dependent, ordinary, and manipulable. The text also gives us a subtle challenge here to human autonomy. People want gods they can produce from the leftovers of their own world. They prefer a deity emerging from familiar resources to the living God who confronts and rules them. The idol is comforting precisely because it has no sovereignty. But that comfort is false. A warmed body and baked bread are real blessings; a self-made god is a lie.

Here Isaiah refuses vagueness and makes the spiritual issue concrete. Idols are not harmless mistakes; they train dependence on what has no life. The warning is stern because covenant mercy is not casual.

Isaiah 44:16

Half the wood is burned in the fire. Over it a man roasts meat, eats, is satisfied, and says, “Aha, I am warm, I have seen the fire!” Isaiah lingers over these practical uses to press the absurdity. The cooked meal and bodily warmth are real effects. The wood functions as wood. It gives heat, not salvation. None of that is wrong. The problem comes when the same material is then assigned divine status. By stressing satisfaction and warmth, Isaiah shows that humans already receive from creation what creation is meant to give. Trouble begins when they demand from it what only God can give. That is a contemporary word. Many created gifts truly comfort and serve us. Food nourishes. Relationships gladden. Work can bless. Beauty can refresh. The lie enters when we ask any of them to become godlike—to define us, save us, justify us, or secure us absolutely. What was meant to warm us then begins to rule us. Isaiah’s satire is careful and profound. The issue is not enjoying created things. It is worshiping them.

Isaiah 44:17

Then the rest is made into a god. The man bows down, worships, and prays, “Deliver me, for you are my god!” Here the full irrationality is exposed. The leftover becomes savior. The prayer itself is revealing. What the idolater wants is deliverance. At the deepest level, false worship grows out of a real human need. People long to be rescued, secured, preserved. Isaiah does not mock that desire. He shows how tragically it is misdirected. The plea “deliver me” is right in instinct and wrong in object. That is the heart of idolatry. It takes the soul’s true cry and lays it at the feet of what cannot answer. The rest of the wood cannot rescue because it is still wood. It has changed place in the man’s imagination, not nature in reality. Isaiah's wording should awaken compassion as well as clarity. Idolaters are not always merely arrogant. They are often pleading creatures asking salvation from powerless substitutes. That makes false worship both foolish and pitiable. The answer is not contempt but the revelation of the true Savior.

Isaiah 44:18

“They know not, nor do they discern, for he has shut their eyes… and their hearts.” Whether the hardening is viewed as divine judgment, the effect of idolatry itself, or both, the result is awful: blindness of eye and hardness of heart. Idolatry is more than a bad conclusion reached by open-minded people. It is a condition of distorted perception. Something has happened to sight and understanding. This explains why the absurdity in the previous verses is not obvious to the idolater. Spiritual deception makes contradictions livable. The eyes may function physically, yet truth is hidden. The heart may reason in ordinary matters, yet fail to discern the deepest reality. This should make us humble. Apart from grace, we are not neutral observers naturally inclined toward truth. We are capable of loving and defending illusion. It should also make us earnest in prayer. Since blindness runs deeper than information, argument alone will never suffice. God must open eyes. Yet the verse also warns. Repeated false worship darkens perception further. A lie embraced for comfort ends by reshaping the mind itself. What begins as refusal becomes inability.

Isaiah 44:19

No one stops to consider the absurdity: half the wood was burned, bread baked on the coals, meat roasted and eaten—shall the rest become an abomination to bow before? Isaiah emphasizes the missing act: reflection. The idolater does not bring the matter to heart. He does not sit with what he has done long enough for truth to break in. This is spiritually acute. Many forms of false worship survive because people refuse sustained, honest reflection. They rush from desire to devotion without stopping to ask what they are actually trusting. Sin loves haste. Idolatry thrives where the heart does not examine itself before God. The prophet describes the missing knowledge and discernment not to flatter rationalism, but to urge moral thoughtfulness. Here are moments when deliverance begins simply by asking the right question and refusing to flee from it. “What am I bowing to? What have I made ultimate? What, in truth, is this thing?” The line “a block of wood” is intentionally plain. Naming the idol accurately is part of unmasking it. The expression of reverence dies when truth enters.

Isaiah 44:20

“He feeds on ashes; a deceived heart has turned him aside.” That is one of Isaiah’s sharpest pictures of spiritual futility. Ashes cannot nourish. They are the residue of what is burned up and empty. Yet the idolater feeds on them, meaning he sustains his inner life with what has no life in it. A deceived heart lies at the center. The issue is more than mistaken technique or insufficient evidence. The heart itself has been led astray. Therefore the person cannot deliver himself or even ask the necessary question, “Is there not a lie in my right hand?” That last phrase is striking. The lie is held close, grasped, possessed. Falsehood becomes intimate. Many people are less enslaved by what they publicly defend than by what they privately clutch. This sentence speaks powerfully to self-deception. Apart from grace, the heart can be so turned that it cannot diagnose its own hunger properly. It consumes ash and calls it food. Yet the question at the end hints at hope. Deliverance begins when God gives enough light to ask whether what I hold is a lie.

Isaiah 44:21

“Remember these things, O Jacob, and Israel, for you are my servant; I formed you; you are my servant; O Israel, you will not be forgotten by me.” After the long exposure of idols, the Lord turns back to his people with covenant tenderness. Remembering becomes the key. They are to remember both the emptiness of false gods and their own identity under God. Repetition of “my servant” drives home belonging. The people are tempted by lies, but they are not defined by lies. They are formed by the Lord. The final assurance, that Israel will not be forgotten, is especially sweet. The people might forget him, as the chapter has shown. He will not forget them. That is not indulgence. It is steadfast covenant love. Memory in Scripture is relational. To be remembered by God is to be held in his active mercy. Here Isaiah invites the wandering heart back by reminding it that it already belongs to the Lord who made it. The strongest antidote to idolatry is not bare shame alone. It is remembering whose servant you are and how steadfastly he remembers you.

Isaiah 44:22

The Lord declares that he has blotted out transgressions like a cloud and sins like mist; therefore, “Return to me, for I have redeemed you.” The order is beautiful and important. He does not say, “Return, and then perhaps I will redeem.” He says, in effect, “Return because I have redeemed.” Grace leads repentance; it does more than reward it. The metaphor of cloud and mist is fitting. What once darkened and obscured is dispersed by God’s own action. Sin is real, but it need not remain as a permanent covering. The blotting out is decisive. Yet that mercy creates a claim upon the heart. “Return to me.” Forgiveness is not permission to wander farther. It is the opening of the way home. The line guards both grace and repentance from distortion. Grace is free and prior. Repentance is necessary and personal. The Lord’s redemption creates the possibility and the summons of return. Many people stay away because they believe forgiveness depends on achieving worthiness first. God answers that lie here. Come back because I have already acted as Redeemer. Mercy itself is calling you home.

This is one of those places where Isaiah compresses a large burden into a few words. Here the Servant bears the weight others could not bear, joining justice, mercy, and substitution in one costly work. This is the kind of mercy that steadies the weak and unsettles the proud.

Isaiah 44:23

Heaven, earth, mountains, forest, and every tree are summoned to sing because the Lord has redeemed Jacob and will be glorified in Israel. The scale of praise matches the scale of redemption. Creation itself is called to celebrate because God’s restoring work has significance beyond one people’s private comfort. When the Lord redeems, his glory shines in a way that all creation may rightly acknowledge. The movement from cloud-like sins being blotted out to cosmic song is entirely fitting. Forgiveness is not a small thing. It reorders the world around the triumph of God’s mercy. The mention of trees is especially striking after the idol satire. The trees once misused for false worship are now restored to their proper role in creation’s praise. That is the Lord’s way. He not just forgives people; he reclaims creation’s voice from idolatrous misuse. The text should enlarge our sense of redemption. To be forgiven and brought back to God is not a narrow religious event. It is part of the great design by which God fills heaven and earth with his glory. Song is therefore the right response.

Isaiah 44:24

The Lord now speaks again as Redeemer, the one who formed Israel from the womb, and immediately widens the horizon: he is the maker of all things, stretching out the heavens alone and spreading out the earth by himself. The pairing matters. Israel’s Redeemer is the world’s Creator. Therefore his promise of restoration is not fragile or local. The same Lord who formed his people also formed the cosmos. “Alone” and “by myself” cut against every idea of rival assistance. No other deity contributed to creation. That means no other power can now frustrate his redemptive purpose. God’s fidelity to Israel is backed by absolute universal sovereignty. This is why comfort in Isaiah is never sentimental. It is theological to the core. Fear is addressed by showing who God is. If the Redeemer were not the Creator, hope would be uncertain. But because the One who claims Israel is the One who spread out heaven and earth, no promised restoration is beyond his power. Personal redemption and cosmic sovereignty meet in the same God.

Isaiah 44:25

He frustrates the signs of liars, makes diviners mad, turns wise men back, and makes their knowledge foolish. Isaiah moves from creator to revealer. Since the Lord alone governs history, all rival claims to hidden knowledge are exposed. The issue is not that human beings know nothing in any field. It is that human wisdom, divination, and false signs become folly when they presume to explain or control the future apart from God. In Babylon especially, expert interpretation of omens and signs carried enormous prestige. God says he overturns it. He is not impressed by the confidence of the age. This is comforting for believers living under the pressure of celebrated worldly certainty. Systems, forecasts, and elites may speak with great assurance, but none of them sits above the word of the Lord. Their knowledge becomes nonsense the moment it claims ultimacy. Isaiah's wording does not encourage contempt for ordinary learning. It exposes pretensions to final wisdom. God alone can read history from the inside, because God alone writes it. Where human wisdom opposes his word, it eventually collapses under its own emptiness.

Isaiah 44:26

By contrast, the Lord confirms the word of his servant and fulfills the counsel of his messengers. He says of Jerusalem, “She shall be inhabited,” and of the cities of Judah, “They shall be built.” The ruins will rise. The contrast with the previous verse is powerful. False predictions fail; God’s word stands. His servants and messengers do not create the future by their speech. They speak what he has given, and he himself brings it to pass. Jerusalem’s restoration therefore rests not in patriotic longing but in divine faithfulness. The city whose destruction seemed to speak final abandonment will again host life. The towns of Judah, reduced to waste places, will be rebuilt. The text is not the language of vague spiritual comfort only. God’s redemption enters place, community, and history. He cares about inhabitable life. He rebuilds what judgment laid waste. Yet the deeper point remains his word. Restoration happens because he says so. For people staring at ruins—literal or inward—this matters greatly. The rebuilding of life does not begin in human optimism but in the confirming power of God over his own promise.

Isaiah 44:27

The Lord says to the deep, “Be dry; I will dry up your rivers.” The language echoes creation and exodus all at once. The deep in Scripture often evokes what is untamable, overwhelming, and beyond human control. Yet God commands it. Waters that terrify others obey him. This supports the restoration just promised. If seas and depths answer to his word, then the path of redemption will not be blocked by barriers that seem impossible. The exodus background is especially strong here. The God who made a way through the sea remains the God who can dry what must be dried for his people to pass. But there is more. The drying up of hostile waters can also symbolize the humbling of proud powers whose strength and wealth seemed like abundant rivers. Either way, the point is the same: nothing fluid, vast, or apparently uncontrollable stands outside his authority. For fearful hearts, this is medicine. What appears deep and overwhelming to us is not deep to him. He can speak a word and change the whole landscape of danger.

Isaiah 44:28

At last the ruler long hinted at is named: Cyrus. God calls him “my shepherd,” says he shall fulfill all my purpose, and links his work to Jerusalem being rebuilt and the temple founded. The title “shepherd” is surprising, but it highlights function rather than spiritual maturity. Cyrus will serve God’s pastoral purpose toward his people by opening the way for restoration. He is not the savior; he is the instrument. God’s hand stays primary. “All my purpose” is decisive. Cyrus does not function on independent terms. His role, however impressive, is encompassed within God’s larger plan. The mention of temple foundation is especially significant. Restoration is not complete with political release or urban rebuilding alone. Worship must be restored. The place of God’s name among his people matters. Here Isaiah looks beyond mere return to renewed covenant life. It also reminds us how free God is in choosing instruments. He can lay hold of unexpected rulers and make them serve outcomes larger than they know. History may be full of human intentions, but beneath them God pursues his own purpose unfailingly.
