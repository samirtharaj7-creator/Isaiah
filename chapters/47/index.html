Isaiah 47: Comfort and the God Who Carries

Isaiah Chapter 47

Isaiah announces comfort, unmasks idols, and reveals the Lord as Creator, Redeemer, and the One who carries His people home.

Isaiah 47:1-7

Babylon Brought Down From the Throne

Isaiah 47:8-11

Secure Wickedness Suddenly Judged

Isaiah 47:12-15

Sorceries Unable to Save

Isaiah 46 Isaiah 48

Study Notes

Isaiah 47:1

Babylon is summoned to descend “into the dust.” The city that sat like royalty over conquered nations will lose its throne and occupy the place of mourning, defeat, and humiliation. Isaiah personifies the empire as a pampered young woman whose assumed dignity is abruptly stripped away.

“Virgin daughter of Babylon” reflects the city’s proud self-image—untouched, secure, and invincible. Yet the title is filled with irony. Babylon’s outward splendor cannot conceal its violence, idolatry, and corruption. God’s judgment brings hidden character into the open.

“There is no throne” announces more than a change of government. The empire’s claim to permanence is rejected. Babylon has acted as though dominion belonged to it by right, but its authority has always been temporary and accountable to God. The throne from which it oppressed others cannot protect it when the Judge summons it down.

It will no longer be called “tender and delicate.” Luxury had insulated Babylon from the suffering it inflicted. Its leaders could enjoy ease while captives bore heavy burdens. Judgment reverses those conditions and forces the proud city to experience vulnerability.

Later Scripture uses Babylon as a symbol of organized opposition to God, especially when religious deception, political power, luxury, and persecution combine. The principle remains: no system that exalts itself above God and crushes His people can remain enthroned forever. The fall begins with God’s command. The same voice that comforts Zion is able to bring Babylon to the dust.

Isaiah 47:2

The fallen queen is ordered to “take the millstones, and grind meal.” Grinding grain by hand was exhausting domestic labor commonly associated with servitude. Babylon, once served by conquered peoples, is now pictured performing the work of a slave. The reversal is deliberately severe.

“Uncover thy locks, make bare the leg, uncover the thigh” describes the removal of royal finery and the exposure involved in forced captivity. The city that clothed itself in magnificence will cross rivers without the protection or dignity it once assumed. Isaiah is not inviting sinful curiosity; he is portraying the public disgrace of an arrogant power brought low.

The imagery answers Babylon’s cruelty. It had reduced nations to servitude and treated human beings as objects of imperial gain. Now its own experience reflects the humiliation it imposed. God’s judgment is morally related to the evil being judged.

The verse also exposes the fragility of status. Clothing, titles, wealth, and ceremony can create the appearance of importance, but none changes the creature’s dependence upon God. Babylon’s identity rested upon being seen as untouchable. Once the Lord removes its power, the appearance collapses.

Scripture does not condemn ordinary labor; the shame lies in the reversal from boastful domination to compulsory service. The one who would not use power mercifully loses the power it abused. Every authority should therefore be exercised with humility, remembering that God hears the oppressed and can bring rulers, institutions, and nations to account.

Isaiah 47:3

“Thy nakedness shall be uncovered, yea, thy shame shall be seen.” Babylon’s judgment includes exposure. The empire maintained an image of wisdom, beauty, wealth, and security, but beneath that image lay pride, occult deception, and merciless oppression. God will reveal publicly what imperial propaganda concealed.

Exposure is a recurring feature of divine judgment. Sin prefers secrecy and carefully managed appearances. Babylon believed that its practices could remain hidden and its power could prevent accountability. The Lord declares that neither is true. What has been covered will be uncovered before the Judge.

“I will take vengeance” does not describe uncontrolled passion. Divine vengeance is God’s righteous response to persistent evil, particularly evil that has injured the helpless and dishonored His name. Babylon’s violence has created cries that justice must answer. The Lord’s judgment assures victims that cruelty will not be permitted to define the final outcome.

The KJV phrase “I will not meet thee as a man” is difficult, but its force is clear within the passage: Babylon will not face a merely human opponent who can be intimidated, deceived, bribed, or outmaneuvered. The empire must answer to God. Its political skill and magical arts will offer no protection before Him.

The verse is therefore both warning and comfort. It warns that no secret sin remains permanently hidden and no human strength can cancel divine accountability. It comforts the afflicted by declaring that the Judge sees the shameful deeds committed against them and will act. God’s vengeance is not permission for personal revenge; it is a reason to entrust justice to Him.

Isaiah 47:4

The taunt against Babylon pauses for a confession of faith: “As for our redeemer, the LORD of hosts is his name, the Holy One of Israel.” In the middle of judgment, Israel remembers who stands on its side. Babylon may appear to own the captives, but their true Redeemer has not surrendered His claim.

“Redeemer” carries the idea of a near relative who acts to recover what has been lost, protect the vulnerable, and restore family rights. Israel cannot free itself from Babylon, but it belongs to One who is both willing and able to intervene. His nearness is covenantal, while His power is universal.

“The LORD of hosts” identifies Him as commander over all heavenly and earthly forces. Babylon’s armies do not face a weak tribal deity. The Lord rules the hosts of heaven and directs the movements of nations. The title places the empire’s impressive military power in proper proportion.

He is also “the Holy One of Israel.” His saving action is inseparable from His holiness. He redeems His people not so they may return unchanged to rebellion, but so they may belong to Him as a holy people. The same holiness that judges Babylon also corrects Israel.

This brief declaration turns attention from the enemy’s fall to the character of the Savior. Deliverance is secure not because the captives possess strength but because their Redeemer has an invincible name. Believers facing powers greater than themselves may likewise confess that their hope rests in the Lord’s relationship, authority, and holiness—not in their ability to overcome oppression alone.

Isaiah 47:5

Babylon is told, “Sit thou silent, and get thee into darkness.” The city once filled with royal commands, celebrations, and confident predictions will be reduced to silence. Darkness replaces the brilliance with which it impressed the nations. Its voice and visibility disappear together.

Silence is particularly fitting because Babylon’s claims have been loud. It boasted of permanence, unrivaled authority, and secret wisdom. Yet when God’s judgment arrives, the empire has no valid defense. Its astrologers cannot explain the disaster, its gods cannot speak, and its rulers cannot reverse the decree.

“Thou shalt no more be called, The lady of kingdoms” removes the title by which Babylon understood itself. It had exercised influence over many peoples and imagined that supremacy belonged to it permanently. God declares that its identity as mistress of the nations is ending.

The verse warns against building identity upon position. Babylon did not merely possess authority; it made authority the basis of its self-exaltation. When the position vanished, nothing remained to support the proud title. Human beings and institutions are spiritually endangered when they cannot imagine themselves apart from power, admiration, or control.

Darkness here is not God’s final purpose for the oppressed but His judgment upon the oppressor. Zion will eventually be called to arise and shine, while Babylon enters obscurity. God reverses the false arrangements produced by sin. Those who silence others may themselves be silenced, while those whose cries seemed unheard discover that their Redeemer has been listening.

Isaiah 47:6

God explains that He was angry with His people, allowed His inheritance to be profaned, and gave Judah into Babylon’s hand. The exile was not evidence that Babylon’s gods had defeated the Lord. It occurred within His judgment upon covenant rebellion.

Yet being used as an instrument of judgment did not excuse Babylon’s conduct. “Thou didst shew them no mercy.” The empire exceeded any rightful role and delighted in domination. It treated the defeated not as human beings under discipline but as objects to exploit.

“Upon the ancient hast thou very heavily laid thy yoke” singles out the elderly, who were especially unable to bear harsh treatment. God notices not only national policies but the suffering of particular vulnerable people. The strength of a society is morally tested by how it treats those who cannot defend themselves.

This verse holds together divine sovereignty and human responsibility. God can permit a nation to accomplish judgment while still condemning that nation’s pride, motives, and cruelty. Babylon cannot defend itself by saying that its success proved divine approval. Permission is not the same as moral endorsement.

The same principle applies to every form of authority. Possessing power or achieving success does not establish righteousness. Those who become instruments of correction, leadership, or discipline remain accountable for the spirit and manner in which they act. God’s own judgment is measured and purposeful; human cruelty distorts it. Babylon will fall because it turned granted power into merciless oppression.

Isaiah 47:7

Babylon said, “I shall be a lady for ever.” Its present success seemed so complete that decline became unimaginable. The city confused a season of power with a guarantee of permanence.

“Thou didst not lay these things to thy heart” identifies the spiritual failure beneath the boast. Babylon saw conquered cities, flowing wealth, and submissive nations, but did not reflect upon moral accountability. It failed to ask where its course was leading or how God viewed its treatment of others.

“Neither didst remember the latter end of it” calls attention to consequences. Wisdom does not judge a path only by its immediate pleasures or advantages. It asks what that path produces. Pride lives in the present moment and assumes that tomorrow must preserve today’s success.

Babylon had also forgotten the lesson written into the rise and fall of earlier powers. Empires that appear permanent are still mortal. A nation may dominate its neighbors and yet be moving toward judgment. No accumulation of influence can exempt it from the moral government of God.

The warning is personal as well as political. Sin often persuades people to consider only the present benefit—approval, pleasure, profit, or control—while refusing to contemplate the end. Scripture repeatedly urges the heart to remember that choices mature into character and character moves toward a final outcome.

True security does not come from saying, “I shall be…for ever.” It comes from belonging to the One who truly is eternal. Humility remembers that every earthly privilege is temporary and must be exercised under God’s judgment.

Isaiah 47:8

Babylon is described as “given to pleasures” and dwelling “carelessly.” The carelessness is a false sense of security, not peaceful trust. The city believes that wealth, walls, military power, and secret arts have placed it beyond danger.

Its inward boast—“I am, and none else beside me”—imitates language that belongs to God alone. Babylon does not merely claim political superiority; it behaves as though it were self-sufficient and ultimate. Pride reaches its most dangerous form when a creature assumes the independence of the Creator.

“I shall not sit as a widow, neither shall I know the loss of children” denies the possibility of bereavement. Widowhood and childlessness symbolize the loss of protection, continuity, and future. Babylon imagines that it will never experience the grief it has caused other peoples.

Pleasure becomes spiritually destructive when it numbs the heart to suffering and accountability. Enjoyment itself is not condemned, but Babylon’s luxury is built upon exploitation and accompanied by indifference. Its comfort prevents repentance because it mistakes favorable circumstances for moral safety.

The verse anticipates the later biblical portrait of spiritual Babylon saying, “I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow” (Revelation 18:7). In both cases, proud security ends suddenly.

God calls His people to a different rest. Faithful security acknowledges dependence, receives blessings gratefully, and remains awake to judgment. The safest place is not where sorrow appears impossible, but where the heart humbly trusts the Lord and walks in His ways.

Isaiah 47:9

The very losses Babylon denied will come “in a moment in one day.” Suddenness overturns its illusion of control. Widowhood and the loss of children represent the collapse of both present support and future continuation. The empire will discover that its security was never absolute.

“They shall come upon thee in their perfection” means that the judgment will arrive fully, not as a minor disturbance from which Babylon can quickly recover. The city that brought complete devastation upon others will experience a decisive reversal.

Its “sorceries” and “enchantments” cannot prevent the loss. Babylon had cultivated occult practices as means of gaining hidden knowledge and influencing events. Yet no amount of spiritual manipulation can overrule the living God. On the night the city fell, its wise men could not interpret the divine message until God’s servant was called, and they could not save the kingdom after the warning was understood.

The verse does not treat occult involvement as harmless curiosity. Such practices promise insight and control apart from obedient dependence upon God. They replace prayerful trust with attempts to master unseen powers. What appears to offer security finally deepens deception.

The suddenness also warns against postponing repentance. Long periods without visible judgment may encourage the thought that judgment will never come. Babylon’s fall proves that delay is not cancellation. When the appointed moment arrives, the structures of pride can collapse with astonishing speed. The proper response is not fearful speculation but present faithfulness before God.

Isaiah 47:10

Babylon “trusted” in wickedness. Evil was not merely practiced; it became a source of confidence. The city believed that deception, violence, secret planning, and manipulation could preserve its position.

“None seeth me” reveals the assumption that hidden wrongdoing escapes accountability. Babylon may conceal decisions from the nations and protect its rulers from human scrutiny, but nothing is hidden from God. Secrecy can prevent immediate exposure; it cannot erase moral reality.

“Thy wisdom and thy knowledge, it hath perverted thee.” Knowledge is a gift when it leads to humility, service, and truth. It becomes corrupting when it persuades the knower that superior information removes the need for God. Babylon’s learning did not make it wise because it was separated from righteousness.

The inward statement “I am, and none else beside me” is the fruit of that perversion. The city’s knowledge has closed it within itself. It recognizes no authority above its own judgment and no power beyond its control. The claim resembles practical atheism even when surrounded by religion.

There is a warning here for every culture that prizes information. Intellectual achievement, technology, and expertise can accomplish much, but none can determine ultimate good or remove accountability to the Creator. Knowledge without moral submission may increase the efficiency of evil rather than cure it.

True wisdom begins by acknowledging that God sees, God knows, and God alone is ultimate. The heart is freed from self-deception when it stops using knowledge to justify independence and receives truth as a call to obedience.

Isaiah 47:11

“Therefore shall evil come upon thee.” The judgment is connected directly to Babylon’s pride, cruelty, and false trust. Its fall is not a meaningless accident. The moral order of God’s government answers persistent rebellion.

The KJV says Babylon will not know “from whence it riseth,” while the underlying expression may also carry the thought that it will know no charm capable of removing it. Both ideas fit the passage. The city will be surprised by the disaster, and its magical practices will be powerless against it.

“Thou shalt not be able to put it off” destroys the illusion that every crisis can be managed. Babylon has survived previous dangers through wealth, strategy, and influence, but the appointed judgment cannot be negotiated away. Human ingenuity has reached its limit.

“Desolation shall come upon thee suddenly, which thou shalt not know” is especially striking because God has announced the fall beforehand. Babylon does not know, not because no warning exists, but because pride prevents it from receiving the warning. Information rejected does not become saving knowledge.

The same danger appears whenever people assume that familiarity with spiritual truth equals readiness. A prophecy may be heard, a warning understood, and yet the heart remain unprepared because it refuses repentance.

God reveals coming judgment not to satisfy curiosity but to awaken faithfulness. The wise response is not to calculate how long rebellion can continue safely. It is to seek the Lord while mercy is offered. Babylon’s sudden fall teaches that no refuge remains when every warning has been converted into another occasion for pride.

Isaiah 47:12

The Lord speaks with solemn irony: “Stand now with thine enchantments, and with the multitude of thy sorceries.” Babylon is invited to rely upon the very practices it has trusted since its youth. The challenge is not an endorsement but an exposure.

“Perhaps thou shalt be able to profit, perhaps thou mayest prevail” reveals how empty the confidence has become. What Babylon treated as certainty is reduced to a desperate possibility. At the decisive moment, its spiritual advisers can offer only uncertainty.

Long practice does not transform falsehood into truth. Babylon has devoted generations to enchantments, omens, and occult knowledge. Tradition may make error familiar and institutionally respected, but age cannot give saving power to what God has condemned.

The verse also exposes the human desire to control the future. Sorcery promises access to power without surrender to God’s will. It seeks knowledge while avoiding obedience and influence without moral accountability. In contrast, biblical faith entrusts the unknown future to the known character of God.

When a false refuge begins to fail, people often cling to it more intensely. Babylon does not immediately repent; it multiplies the methods that have deceived it. The Lord’s irony presses the city to recognize that nothing within its established system can save it.

A crisis can therefore become a moment of grace if it reveals the inadequacy of long-trusted substitutes. The right response is not to practice the old enchantments more earnestly but to abandon them and turn to the living God.

Isaiah 47:13

“Thou art wearied in the multitude of thy counsels.” Babylon possesses many advisers, systems, and interpretations, yet abundance has produced exhaustion rather than clarity. When counsel is separated from truth, multiplying opinions may deepen confusion.

The astrologers, stargazers, and monthly prognosticators studied the heavens in an effort to discover and control what was coming. God had created the stars to declare His glory and mark seasons, but Babylon turned creation into an instrument of divination. It sought guidance from the heavens while refusing the Lord of heaven.

The challenge is direct: “Let them stand up, and save thee.” Prediction is tested by deliverance. Even accurate observation of natural patterns cannot provide salvation from divine judgment. The advisers may describe movements in the sky, but they cannot change the moral government of God.

The verse distinguishes astronomy from astrology. Careful study of creation may honor the Creator; assigning destiny and spiritual authority to created bodies does not. The problem is not looking at the stars but asking them to provide what belongs to God—ultimate guidance, security, and knowledge of the future.

Babylon’s weariness also speaks to the burden of life without revelation. People move from one theory to another, hoping the next explanation will supply certainty. The Lord’s word offers a different foundation. It does not answer every curiosity, but it reveals enough to know God, understand His will, and walk faithfully.

Counsel becomes saving wisdom only when it brings the heart into submission to the One who rules both stars and nations.

Isaiah 47:14

Babylon’s advisers are “as stubble.” The image removes every appearance of strength. Before the fire of judgment, those who seemed wise and influential possess no more resistance than dry straw.

“They shall not deliver themselves from the power of the flame.” The people who promised protection cannot protect even themselves. This is the final test of false spiritual authority. Those who claim secret access to power are exposed as helpless when the true God acts.

Isaiah adds that this is “not a coal to warm at, nor fire to sit before.” The fire is not a small, manageable flame that Babylon can use for comfort. It represents destructive judgment, not domestic warmth. The city cannot turn God’s decree into another resource for its own purposes.

The distinction is spiritually important. God’s presence is warmth, light, and refuge to those who trust Him, but persistent rebellion encounters His holiness as consuming judgment. The difference does not lie in a changeable God but in the relationship of the person to His righteous character.

Stubble also suggests finality. Once consumed, it does not rise again as an oppressive power. Scripture’s judgment imagery points toward the decisive removal of evil, not its endless preservation. God will not allow Babylonian cruelty to continue forever.

Those who build upon truth need not fear the collapse of deception. But anyone resting in false counsel should hear the mercy within the warning. There is still time to leave the stubble and seek refuge in the Lord before the flame reveals what cannot endure.

Isaiah 47:15

“So shall they be unto thee with whom thou hast laboured.” Babylon’s long investment in astrologers, sorcerers, advisers, and commercial partners yields no rescue. The system to which it devoted energy cannot stand beside it in judgment.

“Thy merchants, from thy youth” points to the economic networks that strengthened the city. Commerce itself is not condemned, but Babylon used wealth to reinforce pride, luxury, and domination. Relationships organized chiefly around profit prove fragile when prosperity disappears.

“They shall wander every one to his quarter.” When Babylon falls, its partners seek their own safety. Alliances based on advantage dissolve once the advantage is gone. The city that appeared to stand at the center of the world discovers that its admirers were not faithful friends.

“None shall save thee” is the chapter’s final verdict. Gods, magic, wisdom, wealth, merchants, and political status have all been examined and found powerless. Babylon’s tragedy is not that salvation was impossible in every sense, but that it persisted in seeking salvation everywhere except in God.

The verse invites an honest examination of our own refuges. Who or what remains when success disappears? Relationships of genuine love may endure hardship, but idols of usefulness and prestige do not. Earthly resources have proper roles, yet none can bear the ultimate demand of the soul.

Isaiah leaves Babylon without a human savior so that the contrast with Israel’s Redeemer may remain clear. Zion’s hope is not superior wealth or counsel. It is the Holy One who alone can say, “I will carry, and will deliver.”
