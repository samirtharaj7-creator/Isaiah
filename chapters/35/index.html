Isaiah 35: Woes, Trust, and Restoration

Isaiah Chapter 35

Isaiah rebukes false confidence and calls Judah to trust the Lord, ending with a vision of wilderness joy and restored creation.

The Highway of Holiness and Redeemed Joy

Study Notes

Isaiah 35:1

After Edom’s measured desolation, Isaiah opens a new world with joy. The wilderness and solitary place rejoice, and the desert blossoms abundantly. The abrupt contrast is deliberate. Judgment is not God’s final delight; it clears the stage for the restoration of life and the homecoming of the redeemed.

The land is personified as rejoicing before any human traveler appears. Creation itself participates in salvation. Where chapter 34 showed thorns, burning ground, and abandoned ruins, chapter 35 shows beauty arising from barrenness. God does not merely remove enemies; He heals the world their rebellion has wounded.

The precise flower behind the KJV’s “rose” is uncertain, but identification is not essential. The wonder lies in bloom where no bloom was expected. The desert does not slowly congratulate itself for becoming fertile. Life comes because the Creator acts.

This imagery has historical resonance in the restoration of God’s people after devastation, but its reach is larger. Isaiah joins renewed land, bodily healing, holiness, safe pilgrimage, and everlasting joy. The fullness belongs to the messianic kingdom and the renewed creation.

The verse therefore speaks to places and lives that appear spiritually or emotionally barren. It does not promise that every present wilderness will immediately become easy. Biblical hope is not denial of drought. It is confidence that drought does not possess the final word over what God has made.

The wilderness “shall be glad for them” may connect its joy with the redeemed who will travel through it, or simply intensify its own rejoicing. Either way, salvation creates fellowship between people and land rather than escape from creation.

Christ’s resurrection is the decisive pledge of this pattern: life appearing where death had claimed finality. Those who belong to Him await not an eternal desert of disembodied existence but a world made new. The first blossom in Isaiah’s wilderness is therefore a sign that God’s creative mercy is stronger than every ruin judgment has exposed.

Isaiah 35:2

The blossom becomes abundant, and the desert rejoices “even with joy and singing.” Lebanon, Carmel, and Sharon—regions celebrated for forests, fruitful slopes, and beauty—lend their glory to the renewed wilderness. The places that mourned in Isaiah 33 now become measures of restoration.

Yet the climax is not scenery: “they shall see the glory of the LORD, and the excellency of our God.” Creation’s beauty serves revelation. The desert does not become an object of worship; its transformation directs attention to the Creator whose character is displayed in making life flourish.

“Glory” in Isaiah often concerns the manifest weight and splendor of God’s presence. Earlier, His glory terrified the sinful prophet and shook the temple. Here that same glory is seen in saving renewal. Holiness is not only the fire that consumes rebellion; it is the radiance that heals and beautifies what grace has reclaimed.

The pronoun “they” may include the renewed places poetically and the people who behold them. Isaiah’s vision allows creation and redeemed humanity to rejoice together. Salvation overcomes the estrangement introduced by sin.

The lavish language guards against thin expectations. God’s goal is not mere survival after catastrophe. The wilderness receives “the glory of Lebanon” and “the excellency of Carmel and Sharon.” Restoration is generous, beautiful, and worthy of song.

Christian faith should therefore value beauty without making it ultimate. Art, landscape, music, and cultivated abundance can become signs of God’s goodness when received with gratitude and justice. They become idols when the gift hides the Giver or when beauty is purchased through another’s suffering.

The final kingdom will reveal God without distortion. Revelation portrays a city needing no created light because divine glory fills it, yet the renewed creation remains richly itself. Isaiah’s desert keeps its identity while receiving unexpected splendor. Grace does not erase creaturehood; it brings creation into the fullness for which God intended it.

Isaiah 35:3

The vision turns into a command: “Strengthen ye the weak hands, and confirm the feeble knees.” Future hope is meant to change how God’s people treat the exhausted in the present. Those who have heard the promise are commissioned to pass courage to those who can scarcely continue.

Weak hands suggest inability to work, fight, or hold on. Feeble knees suggest instability under prolonged fear. Isaiah does not shame either condition. Weariness is recognized as real, and the answer is communal strengthening rather than accusation.

The plural commands matter. God can strengthen directly, yet He ordinarily makes His people instruments of His encouragement. A congregation that celebrates restoration while ignoring the faint contradicts the prophecy. Hope becomes pastoral action through presence, truthful speech, practical assistance, and patient companionship.

Strengthening is not the same as offering easy optimism. Chapter 34 has faced judgment fully, and the fearful have genuine reasons for trembling. The encourager does not say that danger is imaginary. He announces that God’s coming is more decisive than the danger.

Hebrews applies this language to believers running the race under discipline. Weakness can spread when paths remain crooked; therefore the community is also to make straight paths so that what is lame may be healed rather than turned aside. Encouragement includes removing avoidable obstacles and creating conditions for perseverance.

The command does not make the strong self-sufficient heroes. Those who strengthen today may need to be carried tomorrow. All receive strength from the same Lord. Christian ministry is mutual dependence upon grace, not a permanent division between capable rescuers and deficient recipients.

Isaiah’s future is certain enough to lend strength before it arrives. The returning redeemed have not yet reached Zion, but the promise of their song steadies present knees. Biblical hope is therefore muscular without being harsh. It enables weary people to take the next step because the road has a destination and the Lord Himself is coming.

Isaiah 35:4

The weak are strengthened through a message addressed to anxious hearts: “Be strong, fear not.” The command is grounded in a person and an event—“Behold, your God will come.” Courage is not produced by looking inward for sufficient emotional resources, but by looking toward the God who approaches.

He comes “with vengeance” and “a recompence.” The same language that made chapter 34 solemn becomes comfort here. Divine vengeance is righteous redress against what has oppressed and ruined. God saves the fearful by confronting the evil that makes fear rational.

“He will come and save you” is emphatic and personal. Salvation is not merely a package of improved conditions sent from a distant throne. God gives Himself in action. This promise finds a decisive fulfillment in Christ’s first coming, when the kingdom entered history through His teaching, healing, death, and resurrection. It awaits complete fulfillment at His return, when He judges evil and gathers the redeemed.

The verse does not rebuke every experience of fear as sin. The heart may race because danger, trauma, or weakness is real. Isaiah speaks tenderly to such hearts and gives them a reason not to let fear become lord. Courage can coexist with trembling when trust chooses God’s promise.

Neither should “vengeance” be placed in the hands of the encouraged. They are not told to save themselves by retaliating. God will come. Their task is to strengthen one another and walk the holy road.

The certainty of His coming also prevents cheap comfort. God does not promise merely that circumstances will somehow work out. He promises to set right, repay, and save. The gospel holds mercy and judgment together: sinners are invited to refuge now, while persistent evil is assured an end.

Every Christian word of encouragement should retain this center. We do not strengthen hearts by promising immunity from all immediate pain, but by announcing the faithful God whose advent places a limit upon pain and whose salvation will not fail.

Isaiah 35:5

When God comes, blind eyes are opened and deaf ears unstopped. The promise includes bodily healing and also reverses the spiritual insensibility that has haunted Isaiah’s message. Those who would not see or hear are transformed into a people able to behold God’s glory and receive His word.

Jesus deliberately pointed to such works when answering John the Baptist’s question about whether He was the Coming One. The healings were not isolated wonders performed merely to display power. They were signs that Isaiah’s promised kingdom had entered history in the Messiah.

Literal and spiritual meanings should not be set against each other. Christ opened actual eyes and ears, showing God’s care for embodied persons. Those acts also revealed the deeper need of humanity for perception. A physically sighted person can remain blind to grace, while a blind believer may see the King by faith. The final kingdom brings complete restoration without reducing disability to a symbol of moral failure.

The verse must not be turned into a guarantee that every faithful person will receive immediate healing now. The kingdom is already present in Christ but not yet consummated. Christians pray, care, use appropriate means, and trust God, while waiting for the resurrection when no bodily brokenness remains.

Isaiah’s earlier call had exposed hearts that resisted hearing. Grace now does what command alone could not: it opens. This preserves both responsibility and hope. People are accountable for rejecting light, yet God is able to heal the faculties sin has darkened.

The church anticipates this promise when it makes worship, teaching, and community accessible rather than treating persons with disabilities as marginal illustrations. Those whom Christ welcomes must not be excluded by His people.

Finally, opened senses are directed toward God. Eyes see His glory, and ears hear the good news of His coming. Healing reaches its purpose in communion. The greatest gift is not merely the recovery of a faculty but the ability, in a renewed body, to delight forever in the Lord who restored it.

Isaiah 35:6

The lame person leaps like a deer, the tongue of the mute sings, and waters burst through the wilderness. Human bodies and the land are healed in one movement. Salvation restores mobility, speech, joy, and environment.

The comparison to a deer conveys more than the ability to walk. It portrays vigorous, delighted freedom. Acts 3 echoes the image when a man lame from birth enters the temple walking, leaping, and praising God. His restored body becomes worship.

The mute tongue does not merely speak; it sings. God’s goal is not bare functionality but joyful expression. Those silenced by bodily limitation or oppressive circumstances receive a voice in the redeemed community.

Waters in the desert correspond to these healings. What was locked, dry, and unproductive opens. Isaiah’s imagery recalls the exodus, when God supplied water in the wilderness, and anticipates the new exodus of the redeemed. Christ also uses living-water language for the Spirit, through whom the life of the coming age is already given.

Care is needed not to imply that persons with disabilities are spiritually dry or incomplete in dignity. They bear God’s image fully now. The promise honors embodied life by declaring that every effect of the fall will finally be removed; it does not reduce people to problems.

Jesus’ healings are foretastes, not the whole harvest. Many faithful believers still live with paralysis, speech impairment, chronic pain, or other limitations. Their hope does not depend upon achieving sufficient faith for a present miracle. It rests in Christ, who has defeated death and will raise His people incorruptible.

The verse also connects personal salvation with creation’s renewal. God does not rescue isolated souls from a disposable world. The same power that causes a lame person to leap makes streams flow through desert. Redemption is cosmic and intimate.

Song is therefore the fitting response. Grace gives movement to those who could not move, voice to those who could not speak, and water to ground that could not live. Nothing barren in God’s creation lies beyond His recreating word.

Isaiah 35:7

The “parched ground” becomes a pool and the thirsty land springs of water. Places once inhabited by desert creatures grow grass, reeds, and rushes—plants that require abundant moisture. The transformation is not slight; the ecology of desolation is reversed.

The KJV’s “parched ground” may evoke shimmering heat that looks like water from a distance. What once offered only the appearance of relief becomes a real pool. God’s salvation replaces illusion with substance. False refuges promised water and gave thirst; the Lord creates water where none could be found.

Reeds and rushes ordinarily grow near sustained water, so they testify that the change is not a momentary shower. The Creator establishes conditions in which life can remain. This corresponds to the “sure” water of Isaiah 33:16 and the broad rivers of 33:21.

The verse carries spiritual resonance because thirst frequently represents human need and God’s Spirit is compared to poured water. Yet the material image should retain its force. The renewed earth matters to God. Salvation includes land no longer hostile to life.

The transformation of the creatures’ habitation also links this chapter with the preceding judgment. Wilderness animals marked Edom’s desolation; here their former lair becomes lush growth. The point is not hostility toward the animals but the end of barrenness as the defining condition of the road home.

Those disappointed by false appearances may find special comfort here. Human promises can shimmer attractively at a distance and vanish when approached. God’s promise becomes more substantial as it is fulfilled. He gives not a mirage of pardon, peace, resurrection, or home, but reality.

Present Christian experience includes genuine firstfruits—living water through the Spirit, restored relationships, and transformed character—while still awaiting the full pool in the desert. The verse teaches patience without vagueness. God’s intention is concrete. Thirst will not be managed forever; it will be answered. Where desolation once had a settled habitation, life will take root.

Isaiah 35:8

A highway rises through the renewed wilderness and is named “The way of holiness.” The exiles’ homecoming is not left to trackless desert. God prepares a clear road and defines its character.

“The unclean shall not pass over it” shows that salvation and holiness cannot be separated. The road is not earned by flawless people; those who walk it are the redeemed of the next verse and the ransomed of verse 10. Grace cleanses them and brings them onto the way. Yet grace does not escort cherished uncleanness into Zion.

The middle wording is difficult. The KJV says, “but it shall be for those: the wayfaring men, though fools, shall not err therein.” Other renderings emphasize that the road belongs to the redeemed and that even the simple will not wander. The central assurance is clear: God’s prepared way is safe and intelligible for those He brings home.

This corrects spiritual elitism. The highway is not navigable only by scholars, rulers, or unusually gifted saints. The simplest traveler who follows God’s way need not be lost. Salvation depends upon the faithfulness of the Guide more than the sophistication of the pilgrim.

At the same time, the road has a definite direction. Modern thought often treats sincerity as sufficient regardless of path. Isaiah speaks of a named way shaped by God’s holiness. Christ fulfills this hope by being the Way and by leading His people through His truth and life.

The highway also echoes a new exodus. God once made a path through the sea; now He makes one through wilderness. Later Isaiah will call for the way of the LORD to be prepared. Every obstacle to homecoming yields to His purpose.

Believers walk this road before reaching its visible end. Holiness is not anxious self-salvation but responsive fellowship with the Redeemer. He forgives falls, corrects wandering, and keeps the traveler moving toward Zion. The promise is both demanding and tender: uncleanness cannot own the road, and weakness cannot make the redeemed miss home.

Isaiah 35:9

No lion or ravenous beast threatens the holy highway. Such dangers will not be found there; “the redeemed shall walk there.” The road is not only clear but protected.

Lions represented real danger to ancient travelers and also served as images of violent powers. Their absence means that neither natural threat nor predatory oppression can interrupt the homecoming. The peace envisioned in Isaiah 11 extends to the road itself.

The walkers are “the redeemed.” The word evokes the kinsman-redeemer who assumes responsibility for a relative’s loss or bondage. God does not rescue as a detached benefactor. He binds the people’s cause to His own covenant faithfulness, pays the necessary cost, and brings them back into inheritance.

Christian readers see this redemption centered in Christ, who gave Himself to free sinners from the vain manner of life and from the dominion of sin and death. The price is not silver or gold but His precious blood. His once-for-all sacrifice grounds the certainty of the journey.

Safety here should not be misapplied as a promise that believers will encounter no danger before the final kingdom. The apostles walked with God and suffered violence. Isaiah describes the consummated highway and, by anticipation, the security of God’s saving purpose. Enemies may wound the pilgrim now, but they cannot cancel redemption or inhabit the final road.

The absence of ravenous beasts also reveals the character of Zion. Predation has no place in the redeemed community. Those who walk toward it must not behave like lions toward the weak. Churches contradict the highway when leaders consume rather than shepherd.

“Shall walk there” is simple and profound. Redemption restores movement and belonging. The people are not merely extracted from danger and left without direction; they travel toward home under God’s protection. Every step rests in an accomplished relationship: they walk because they have been redeemed, not in order to persuade the Redeemer to claim them.

Isaiah 35:10

The ransomed of the LORD return and come to Zion with songs. Everlasting joy rests upon their heads; they obtain joy and gladness, while sorrow and sighing flee. The prophecy ends not merely with arrival but with a complete reversal of emotional and communal exile.

“Return” gathers the historical hope of exiles coming home, the spiritual reality of sinners restored to God, and the final gathering of the redeemed. No single return in Israel’s history exhausted the language. Sorrow and sighing continued after every earthly restoration. The fullness awaits resurrection and the renewed creation.

Their song is communal. Salvation does not leave isolated survivors scattered across the wilderness. God gathers a people whose voices join in worship. The mute tongue of verse 6 now belongs to a multitude singing on the road and in Zion.

“Everlasting joy upon their heads” may picture joy like a crown. What once bowed the head in mourning is replaced by visible honor and delight. Joy is not fragile mood dependent upon favorable circumstances; it endures because the causes of sorrow have been removed and God’s presence is secure.

Sorrow and sighing are personified as fugitives. Earlier, threatening nations fled when God arose; now grief itself flees. Scripture does not belittle present sorrow. It promises its end. Every lament offered in faith is heard within a story moving toward a world where lament has completed its work.

The people are “ransomed,” so their joy rests upon costly grace. They did not construct the highway, defeat the predator, heal their bodies, or purchase their freedom. Christ’s sacrifice and victorious life secure the homecoming. Grace therefore excludes boasting while intensifying song.

This final vision is embodied and relational. The hope is not an immortal soul drifting away from earth at death. God raises His people, restores creation, brings them to Zion, and dwells with them. Judgment has removed what destroys; holiness marks the road; joy fills the home.

Isaiah 31 began with people going down to Egypt because fear governed their sight. Isaiah 35 ends with the redeemed coming up to Zion because God has come to save. The last movement belongs to grace: homeward, singing, and without an end.
