Isaiah 48: Comfort and the God Who Carries

Isaiah Chapter 48

Isaiah announces comfort, unmasks idols, and reveals the Lord as Creator, Redeemer, and the One who carries His people home.

Isaiah 48:1-11

Stubborn Israel Refined for God's Name

Isaiah 48:12-16

The First and the Last Speaks

Isaiah 48:17-22

Peace Refused and Redemption Announced

Isaiah 47 Isaiah 49

Study Notes

Isaiah 48:1

The Lord addresses those who bear the name of Israel and “are come forth out of the waters of Judah.” Their ancestry is genuine, and their public confession sounds orthodox. They swear by the name of the Lord and speak of the God of Israel.

Yet the searching qualification follows: “but not in truth, nor in righteousness.” Religious language has become disconnected from religious life. They invoke God’s name without submitting to His character. The problem is not that confession is unnecessary, but that confession without truth has become a mask.

“Truth” includes sincerity and faithfulness; “righteousness” includes conduct consistent with the covenant. Israel cannot rely upon inherited identity while practicing rebellion. Physical descent and sacred vocabulary do not substitute for a heart that listens to God.

The verse confronts formal religion in every age. It is possible to use correct names, defend sound teachings, and belong visibly to God’s community while resisting His will in daily life. Such hypocrisy is especially serious because it uses God’s honor to shield disobedience.

At the same time, the Lord still calls them “the house of Jacob.” His rebuke is covenantal. He exposes false profession because He desires genuine restoration. Grace does not flatter hypocrisy; it tells the truth so that repentance may become possible.

The question is not merely whether we mention God but whether our words correspond to reality. True worship joins confession with trust, doctrine with character, and public identity with private obedience.

Isaiah 48:2

The people “call themselves of the holy city” and “stay themselves upon the God of Israel.” Their claims appear impressive. Jerusalem is associated with God’s presence, and the divine title “the LORD of hosts” acknowledges His universal authority.

Yet the preceding verse has already declared that their profession lacks truth and righteousness. They depend upon the reputation of the holy city while resisting the holiness of its God. Sacred location becomes a substitute for sacred character.

To “stay” upon God ordinarily describes genuine trust, but here the claim is presumptuous. The people expect covenant privileges without covenant faithfulness. They assume that association with Jerusalem guarantees protection regardless of how they live.

This was the same false confidence later condemned when worshippers treated the temple as a charm while practicing injustice. God’s institutions are gifts intended to lead people into communion and obedience; they become idols when used to avoid both.

Church membership, doctrinal heritage, baptism, ministry, and familiarity with Scripture may all be precious. None grants permission to live independently of God. The stronger the profession, the greater the responsibility to reflect the One whose name is confessed.

The full title “the LORD of hosts” quietly overturns their presumption. The God upon whom they claim to rely is not under the control of the city or its ceremonies. He commands heaven’s armies and judges His own people impartially.

Genuine assurance is not weakened by this warning. It is purified. True security rests in God’s mercy received through living faith—a faith that bears the fruit of obedience rather than using grace as protection for rebellion.

Isaiah 48:3

God reminds Israel, “I have declared the former things from the beginning.” Before significant events occurred, He spoke through His prophets. His word was not a later religious interpretation added after the fact; it preceded the fulfillment.

“They went forth out of my mouth, and I shewed them.” Prophecy is personal communication from God. The emphasis is not merely upon information about future events but upon the identity of the Speaker. Fulfilled prediction calls the people to recognize and trust Him.

“I did them suddenly, and they came to pass.” What God had announced eventually appeared with a suddenness that surprised unbelief. Delay between promise and fulfillment did not indicate inability. When the appointed time arrived, events moved according to His word.

The “former things” provide evidence for believing the new things God is about to reveal. Israel should not approach the promise of deliverance as though it had no history of divine faithfulness. God teaches faith through remembered fulfillment.

There is also an implied rebuke. The people had access to the prophetic word, yet events still seemed sudden because they had not taken that word seriously. Hearing without believing leaves a person spiritually unprepared.

Prophecy should therefore produce faithfulness rather than speculation. Its purpose is not to encourage pride in possessing secret knowledge. It confirms that history remains under God’s authority and calls His people to live in obedient readiness. The right response to fulfilled prophecy is not merely, “The prediction was accurate,” but, “The God who spoke is trustworthy, and His present word must be heard.”

Isaiah 48:4

God knew Israel was “obstinate.” The word describes a people who refuse to bend before truth. Their neck is compared to an iron sinew and their brow to brass: the neck will not bow, and the forehead shows no shame.

The imagery exposes two aspects of rebellion. The iron neck resists submission; the brass brow resists conviction. Israel does not merely fail; it defends failure. Correction meets determination, and mercy is treated as an opportunity to continue unchanged.

God’s foreknowledge of this stubbornness explains why He announced events beforehand. He knew the people would otherwise attribute deliverance to idols or their own wisdom. Prophecy removes excuses and confronts self-deception.

Yet the verse also magnifies grace. God called Israel knowing what Israel would become. His faithfulness was never based upon naïve expectations of human perfection. He does not excuse their rebellion, but neither is He surprised into abandoning His saving purpose.

A hardened heart can exist beneath a religious exterior. Stubbornness may appear as strength of conviction, but its true character is revealed by unwillingness to be corrected by Scripture. Faithfulness stands firmly upon truth; obstinacy stands firmly against it.

The Lord’s description invites honest self-examination. Are we teachable when God’s word challenges inherited assumptions, cherished habits, or personal desires? A bowed neck before God is not weakness. It is the posture in which grace can restore us. Brass must become tender conscience, and iron resistance must yield to willing obedience.

Isaiah 48:5

Because Israel was inclined toward idolatry, God declared events “from the beginning” and showed them “before it came to pass.” His advance announcement prevented the people from giving credit to a carved image.

“Mine idol hath done them” would have been a profound distortion. Israel had already demonstrated this tendency when the golden calf was associated with deliverance from Egypt. Human beings can receive unmistakable mercy and then redirect gratitude toward something they have made.

Prophecy protects the glory of God and the spiritual health of His people. If deliverance were attributed to idols, Israel would become more deeply enslaved to the very falsehood from which God sought to free it. Correctly identifying the Savior is therefore part of salvation.

The verse reveals how readily the heart manufactures explanations that preserve rebellion. When God acts in ways that challenge our loyalties, we may reinterpret His gifts so that no repentance is required. Success is credited to talent alone, providence to luck, and answered prayer to the systems in which we already trusted.

God speaks beforehand so that fulfillment may lead to recognition. The connection between word and event declares, “The Lord has done this.” His glory is not vanity; it is truth. Only when the true source of life and deliverance is known can worship be rightly directed.

Gratitude must therefore be disciplined by revelation. We should not merely celebrate blessings but ask who has given them and for what purpose. God’s gifts call us away from idols and into deeper allegiance to Himself.

Isaiah 48:6

“Thou hast heard, see all this.” Israel has received both the prediction and its fulfillment. The evidence is no longer hidden. God therefore asks, “Will not ye declare it?” Those who have witnessed His faithfulness should acknowledge it openly.

The command to declare carries the responsibility of witness. Israel was chosen not merely to enjoy divine acts but to testify that the Lord alone knows, speaks, and saves. Silence after seeing fulfillment would continue the pattern of resistance.

God now announces “new things from this time, even hidden things.” These are not new because He has just conceived them, but because He is now revealing what had been concealed from human knowledge. His purpose advances beyond what the people have previously understood.

The new revelation includes the approaching fall of Babylon, Israel’s release, and the unfolding mission of the Servant. God’s work will not be confined to repeating earlier acts. The One who delivered from Egypt can create a new exodus and ultimately extend salvation to the ends of the earth.

Past faithfulness should prepare believers for new obedience, not imprison them within familiar expectations. Israel must not assume that God can act only as He acted before. His character is unchanging, but His providence is wonderfully free.

The verse joins memory and expectancy. “See all this” looks backward; “I have shewed thee new things” looks forward. Mature faith does both. It remembers enough to trust and listens closely enough to recognize when God’s purpose unfolds in unexpected ways.

Isaiah 48:7

The new things “are created now, and not from the beginning” in the sense that they are newly brought into Israel’s awareness and are about to take historical form. Until God reveals them, they remain beyond human discovery.

“Even before the day when thou heardest them not” emphasizes that Israel cannot claim prior knowledge. The people did not deduce the plan from political developments, nor did their idols announce it. God’s word is the source.

The Lord anticipates the boast, “Behold, I knew them.” Pride wishes to convert revelation into personal achievement. Once an event occurs, people may speak as though it had always been obvious. God removes this possibility by making clear that the new work originates with Him.

The language of creation is significant. Deliverance is not merely a rearrangement of favorable circumstances. God brings into history a possibility that human power could not produce. The exiles see an immovable empire; God creates a road home.

This creative pattern reaches its fullness in the gospel. Salvation is not the natural development of human religion or moral improvement. It is God’s new act in Christ, bringing life where sin had produced death and forming a people who could not save themselves.

Humility is the proper response to divine newness. We receive rather than invent salvation. We listen rather than boast that we already knew. God’s unexpected work does not contradict His earlier revelation; it displays depths within His purpose that human wisdom could never uncover by itself.

Isaiah 48:8

Israel had not heard or known these things beforehand, and its ear “was not opened.” The difficulty was not merely lack of opportunity. The people had repeatedly closed themselves against God’s voice.

The Lord says, “I knew that thou wouldest deal very treacherously.” Israel’s unfaithfulness resembles betrayal within a covenant relationship. It received divine love and privilege but repeatedly transferred loyalty to idols and human alliances.

It was called “a transgressor from the womb.” This does not mean that the nation had no meaningful choices or that repentance was impossible. It describes how deeply rebellion marked its history from the beginning. The golden calf appeared soon after deliverance, and later generations repeated the pattern.

God’s knowledge of their treachery did not prevent Him from revealing His purpose. Grace addresses people whom God fully knows. He does not save because He has mistaken sinners for righteous persons; He saves while exposing the truth about them.

The verse removes grounds for spiritual pride. Israel’s restoration cannot be explained by superior faithfulness. It will be an act of mercy toward a people who have resisted and betrayed the covenant.

Yet grace is not sentimental. God names treachery so that it may be forsaken. A person cannot receive healing while insisting that the wound does not exist. The Lord’s severe diagnosis prepares for the astonishing declaration that He will restrain anger, refine His people, and redeem them for His name’s sake.

Isaiah 48:9

“For my name’s sake will I defer mine anger.” Israel deserves judgment, but God restrains the full expression of wrath. His name represents His revealed character—merciful, gracious, patient, truthful, and holy.

“For my praise will I refrain for thee, that I cut thee not off.” The preservation of Israel is not a reward for covenant faithfulness; the preceding verses have described hypocrisy, stubbornness, idolatry, and treachery. The reason lies in God Himself.

This does not make His action selfish in a human sense. God’s glory is the revelation of what is ultimately true and good. If His saving purpose failed, the nations would misunderstand His character and the promise through which blessing was to reach the world would appear defeated. By preserving and restoring His people, He demonstrates that mercy is stronger than their unworthiness without treating sin as insignificant.

The verse is a clear witness to grace. Salvation begins in God’s character, not human merit. He disciplines because He is holy and preserves because He is merciful. Neither truth cancels the other.

For believers, this provides deep assurance and profound humility. Our hope does not rest upon presenting a flawless record to God. It rests upon His faithful name. Yet the grace that prevents destruction also calls us to bear that name honorably. Those spared for His praise should live so that His patience is not used as permission for further rebellion.

Isaiah 48:10

“Behold, I have refined thee, but not with silver.” Israel’s exile is compared to a furnace. Affliction exposes what prosperity concealed, separates false confidence from genuine faith, and confronts the people with the consequences of rebellion.

The qualification “not with silver” suggests that God has not applied the full intensity used to purify precious metal. If Israel received judgment in exact proportion to its sin, nothing would remain. The refining is measured by mercy.

“I have chosen thee in the furnace of affliction” may also carry the sense of testing or proving. God’s covenant purpose is not abandoned in the furnace. The place that appears to signify rejection becomes the place in which He disciplines, preserves, and prepares a remnant.

Affliction does not automatically purify. Suffering can harden as well as humble. Its refining effect is realized when the sufferer listens to God, relinquishes idols, and allows faith to be reshaped. The furnace reveals; grace transforms.

The verse should not be used to suggest that God delights in pain. His purpose is restoration, not cruelty. He limits the heat and remains present with His people. The same God who permits the furnace declares that He has carried them from the womb and will carry them to old age.

Christians may therefore endure trials without imagining that suffering earns salvation. Refinement does not replace grace; it is one way grace frees the heart from destructive attachments. The Refiner seeks not our ruin but a faith that reflects His character.

Isaiah 48:11

The reason is repeated with unusual emphasis: “For mine own sake, even for mine own sake, will I do it.” Israel’s redemption rests entirely upon God’s character and purpose. The repetition removes every attempt to claim that national merit has compelled Him to act.

“For how should my name be polluted?” If God permanently abandoned His promise, the surrounding nations would conclude that He was unable to save or unfaithful to His word. His restoration of Israel vindicates the truth about who He is.

“I will not give my glory unto another” connects redemption with the conflict against idolatry. Babylon’s gods must not receive credit for history, and Israel must not take credit for grace. The Lord alone foretold, governed, judged, preserved, and delivered.

Divine jealousy for glory is unlike human vanity. God does not compete from insecurity. Since He alone is Creator and Savior, giving ultimate glory elsewhere would establish falsehood and enslave worshippers to what cannot save. His glory is the radiance of reality.

The verse humbles every redeemed person. Salvation cannot become a reason for superiority. Those who are rescued have nothing of which to boast except the Lord. At the same time, it gives assurance: the final foundation of hope is more stable than fluctuating human performance.

Grace received rightly produces a life devoted to God’s honor. We do not obey in order to manufacture a claim upon salvation; we obey because mercy has made us His. The One who acts for His name forms a people who desire that His name be hallowed in them.

Isaiah 48:12

“Hearken unto me, O Jacob and Israel, my called.” Despite their hypocrisy and treachery, God has not forgotten His call. The name “Jacob” recalls weakness and conflict; “Israel” recalls covenant identity. Both belong within the story of grace.

Calling is both privilege and responsibility. God chose Israel to know Him, display His character, preserve His revelation, and become a channel of blessing. Election was never permission for complacency. The called people must listen to the Caller.

“I am he; I am the first, I also am the last.” God stands at the beginning and completion of the story. He existed before the rise of Babylon and will remain after Babylon has fallen. No empire can precede Him, outlast Him, or interrupt His final purpose.

The title later appears in Revelation as part of the divine identity associated with Christ. Within the whole biblical witness, the One who enters history as the Servant shares the eternal sovereignty of God and brings the saving plan to completion.

For Israel, the immediate implication is clear: the future does not belong to Babylon. It belongs to the Lord who is already present at history’s end. His people may feel trapped within one painful chapter, but He holds the whole book.

Listening becomes possible when fear is corrected by this vision. The God who calls is not struggling to survive alongside competing powers. He is the first and the last. Those who belong to Him can obey in the present because their future rests in His eternal faithfulness.

Isaiah 48:13

God’s hand “hath laid the foundation of the earth,” and His right hand “hath spanned the heavens.” The Creator who speaks about political events is the One who called the universe into being. Deliverance from Babylon is not difficult when measured against creation.

The imagery of spanning the heavens suggests effortless authority. What overwhelms human comprehension lies within the reach of God. Earth and heaven are neither divine rivals nor independent forces; they are His workmanship.

“When I call unto them, they stand up together.” Creation responds to its Maker’s command. The ordered heavens become a witness against Israel’s disobedience. Stars and earth fulfill their appointed role, while the called people resist the voice that gave them identity.

The verse also prepares Israel to trust God’s use of Cyrus. The rise and fall of empires may appear chaotic, but the Lord who orders creation can summon historical agents as readily as He summons the heavens. His providence is not limited by geography or political strength.

Creation faith has practical importance. If God is merely a private spiritual comfort, Babylon may seem more powerful in public history. But if He is Creator, every ruler, army, economy, and natural resource remains within His domain.

Believers need not understand how every promise will be fulfilled. They are asked to consider who has spoken. The hand that founded the earth has not grown weak, and the voice that commands heaven is fully able to lead captives home.

Isaiah 48:14

The people are called to “assemble” and answer a question: Which of the idols has declared these things? The false gods remain silent because they neither know the future nor govern it. God’s announcement of Babylon’s fall again becomes evidence of His uniqueness.

“The LORD hath loved him” most naturally points in this context to the chosen agent who will act against Babylon. Divine love here need not describe every aspect of Cyrus’s personal character. It expresses God’s favor in selecting and supporting him for a particular purpose.

“He will do his pleasure on Babylon, and his arm shall be on the Chaldeans.” The conqueror’s action serves the Lord’s pleasure—the just ending of Babylonian oppression and the release of the exiles. Cyrus’s military arm succeeds because God’s sovereign purpose stands behind the historical event.

This does not make conquest itself holy in every detail. Scripture can affirm God’s providential use of a ruler while still maintaining that all rulers remain morally accountable. The central point is that Babylon does not fall by chance and Israel is not released by the power of idols.

The verse asks the hearer to choose between two interpretations of history. One sees events as controlled by visible empires and their gods. The other recognizes the Lord’s word, purpose, and hidden direction.

Faith does not require denying human causes. It recognizes a deeper sovereignty working through them. When God’s pleasure is redemption, no fortress, system, or long-established captivity can finally prevent it.

Isaiah 48:15

“I, even I, have spoken.” The repeated pronoun emphasizes that God Himself stands behind the announcement. The coming deliverance is not rumor, political optimism, or the hopeful imagination of exiles.

“Yea, I have called him.” Cyrus’s appearance on the stage of history is presented as a divine summons. He possesses real ambition and acts through ordinary political and military means, yet his career intersects with God’s purpose for Babylon and Israel.

“I have brought him, and he shall make his way prosperous.” Success is not attributed ultimately to the conqueror’s brilliance. God opens the way necessary for the task He has assigned.

The verse provides an important distinction between an instrument and the Savior. Cyrus is called, brought, and prospered; he is not self-originating. The Lord remains the speaker and director. Israel must not transfer to a human deliverer the worship that belongs to God.

Human agents may be worthy of gratitude without becoming objects of ultimate trust. Leaders, physicians, teachers, pastors, and friends can become channels of providence, but their ability is limited and derived. God alone sees the whole need and governs the larger outcome.

The verse also assures believers that divine calling includes divine provision. This does not promise worldly success for every personal plan. It means that when God determines a redemptive purpose, He is able to prepare the agent, circumstances, and path needed to accomplish it. His word does not depend upon favorable conditions; His providence creates them.

Isaiah 48:16

“Come ye near unto me, hear ye this.” God does not hide His message within secret rites available only to an elite. His saving purpose is publicly proclaimed and invites careful hearing.

“I have not spoken in secret from the beginning.” This sharply contrasts with Babylonian occultism. The Lord’s revelation is morally clear and historically testable. He calls people into faith, not manipulation or esoteric speculation.

The voice then says, “From the time that it was, there am I: and now the Lord GOD, and his Spirit, hath sent me.” The change in expression introduces a divinely sent speaker who has been present within God’s unfolding purpose. Within Isaiah’s developing portrait of the Servant, and in the light of the New Testament, the words find their fullest meaning in Christ—the Sent One who acts in fellowship with the Father and the Spirit.

The verse should not be forced into a technical formula detached from its context, yet its distinction is significant. The Lord GOD sends; His Spirit accompanies the mission; the speaker is sent. Christian faith recognizes here a harmony with the biblical revelation of Father, Son, and Holy Spirit united in the work of salvation.

Christ did not come as an independent teacher presenting private ideas. He was sent by the Father and anointed by the Spirit. His mission brings the publicly declared purpose of God into personal presence.

The command remains, “Come ye near.” Revelation is intended to produce relationship. God makes Himself known so that hearers may leave Babylon’s secrecy and draw near to the One He has sent.

Isaiah 48:17

The speaker identifies the Lord as “thy Redeemer, the Holy One of Israel.” Redemption and holiness remain inseparable. God rescues from bondage so that His people may walk in a way consistent with His character.

“I am the LORD thy God which teacheth thee to profit.” God’s instruction is directed toward genuine good. His commandments are not arbitrary restrictions imposed to diminish life. They reveal the path in which human beings flourish under His loving rule.

“Which leadeth thee by the way that thou shouldest go” presents salvation as guidance. The Lord does more than open the prison door; He leads the redeemed toward their proper destination. Freedom without direction would leave Israel wandering or returning to old bondage.

The tragedy of the chapter is that the people have resisted the Teacher who knows what benefits them. Sin promises profit while producing loss; God’s instruction may require surrender but leads to peace.

In Christ, the Redeemer and Teacher are perfectly revealed. He not only pays the cost of liberation but says, “Follow me.” Grace forgives the wrong path and establishes the believer upon a new one.

Obedience must therefore be understood relationally. It is trusting the wisdom of the One who redeemed us. We do not earn His love by walking in His way; we walk because His love has rescued us and because He knows the way of life.

The verse invites a practical question: Are we willing to be taught, or do we want redemption without guidance? The Holy One offers both pardon and a path, and neither should be separated from the other.

Isaiah 48:18

“O that thou hadst hearkened to my commandments!” The words express divine grief, not cold condemnation. God looks upon the peace Israel forfeited and laments the stubborn refusal that made exile necessary.

“Then had thy peace been as a river.” A river suggests continuing supply, movement, refreshment, and abundance. Biblical peace is more than the absence of conflict. It is wholeness produced by right relationship with God and harmony with His will.

“Thy righteousness as the waves of the sea” portrays strength and constancy. The waves continue arriving, one after another. Had Israel listened, righteous living and God’s saving blessing would have marked its history with enduring fullness.

The verse does not teach salvation by meritorious works. Israel had already been chosen and redeemed by grace. The commandments described the life of a redeemed people. Disobedience did not reveal insufficient earning but broken trust.

God’s law is therefore presented positively. His commandments protect life, justice, worship, family, truth, and human dignity. To reject them is not to gain freedom but to leave the channel through which peace flows.

The lament also reveals that painful consequences are not God’s preferred delight. He desired peace for His people. Their choices mattered, and He did not override them by making rebellion harmless.

Grace still speaks through the regret. The God who says, “O that thou hadst hearkened,” is inviting them to listen now. Past disobedience has cost much, but His teaching and leading remain available to the repentant.

Isaiah 48:19

Had Israel listened, its “seed” would have been “as the sand” and its descendants “like the gravel thereof.” The language recalls the promises to Abraham. Covenant obedience would have allowed the nation to experience the intended fruitfulness and continuity of its calling.

“His name should not have been cut off nor destroyed from before me.” Disobedience placed the people’s future in jeopardy. Exile, death, and scattering were not arbitrary punishments but the historical fruit of abandoning the way of life.

The verse does not mean that every obedient individual is guaranteed a large family or that childlessness proves divine displeasure. The language concerns Israel corporately and the covenant mission entrusted to the nation. God desired a flourishing people through whom His knowledge would spread.

Even after Israel’s failure, God preserves a remnant and advances the promise through the Servant. Human unfaithfulness causes real loss, yet it cannot finally defeat divine grace. The nations will still be reached, not because Israel’s rebellion did not matter, but because God provides a faithful Representative.

The warning remains serious. Choices can forfeit opportunities, diminish witness, and wound future generations. Forgiveness does not always erase every temporal consequence.

At the same time, the promise invites renewed hope. God specializes in bringing life from situations that appear cut off. Those who return to Him become part of a family created not merely by physical descent but by faith in the promised Seed, Christ, through whom Abraham’s blessing reaches the world.

Isaiah 48:20

“Go ye forth of Babylon, flee ye from the Chaldeans.” Deliverance must be acted upon. When God opens the way, His people are not to remain comfortably attached to the place of captivity. Freedom requires departure.

The call to flee does not suggest secret panic. They are to go “with a voice of singing” and proclaim what God has done “even to the end of the earth.” The exodus from Babylon becomes public testimony: “The LORD hath redeemed his servant Jacob.”

Babylon had shaped the exiles’ environment for many years. Some may have built homes, gained stability, or become accustomed to its values. Leaving therefore demanded more than physical movement. It required renewed identity as God’s redeemed people.

Later Scripture applies the call to come out of Babylon to a spiritual system that combines deception, corruption, and opposition to God. The first historical meaning should remain clear, but the principle endures: God’s people must not share the sins of a power He is judging.

Separation is not proud isolation from other human beings. Israel leaves Babylon so that God’s redemption may be announced to the earth. Holiness and mission belong together. The redeemed are separated from false worship and sent toward the world with good news.

The verse asks whether we merely desire relief from consequences or genuine freedom from the values of bondage. God’s call is not only, “Babylon will fall,” but, “Go ye forth.” Faith receives the opened door, leaves what God condemns, and sings about the Redeemer who made release possible.

Isaiah 48:21

“They thirsted not when he led them through the deserts.” The return from Babylon is described in the language of the earlier exodus. The God who guided Israel from Egypt remains able to sustain a new generation on the journey home.

“He caused the waters to flow out of the rock for them.” A barren place cannot prevent divine provision. The rock contains no visible promise of water, yet God brings refreshment from what appears incapable of supplying it.

The repetition—“he clave the rock also, and the waters gushed out”—emphasizes abundance. God does not merely provide a few drops sufficient for survival. His care answers the need fully.

The verse does not promise that the journey will contain no desert. Redemption often leads through difficult territory. The assurance is that God’s presence and provision accompany His people there. A desert with God is safer than Babylon without Him.

The exodus imagery also reaches toward Christ, who gives living water and accompanies His people through the wilderness of this world. The physical miracle becomes a sign of spiritual grace: God can satisfy the thirst that created things cannot reach.

Remembered deliverance strengthens present faith. Israel is called to look back at water from the rock and conclude that the Lord has not lost His power. Believers may do the same with the history of Scripture and personal providence. The form of provision may change, but the Provider remains. Where obedience leads into apparent barrenness, God is able to open resources hidden from human sight.

Isaiah 48:22

“There is no peace, saith the LORD, unto the wicked.” The declaration closes the chapter with sober clarity. God has offered teaching, leading, commandments, redemption, and a road out of Babylon. Persistent wickedness refuses the very conditions in which peace is found.

The verse does not say that the wicked never experience temporary pleasure, success, or outward calm. Babylon had enjoyed all three. Biblical peace is deeper: reconciliation with God, integrity of conscience, moral wholeness, and a future secured in His kingdom. Rebellion cannot produce that peace.

This statement follows the picture of water from the rock. God provides abundantly, yet the wicked remain unsatisfied because they will not receive life on His terms. The problem is not scarcity in God but resistance in the heart.

Peace is not earned as wages for flawless behavior. It is a gift of grace received through faith, but genuine faith turns from wickedness. A person cannot cling to hostility against God while possessing the peace of friendship with Him.

The warning also exposes counterfeit peace. Political stability, religious ceremony, or positive feelings may be called peace even while injustice and idolatry continue. God refuses the false diagnosis. Where wickedness rules, the deepest disorder remains.

The verse invites repentance rather than despair. Its purpose is to drive the hearer away from searching for peace in rebellion. The Redeemer has spoken, taught, led, and opened the way. Peace becomes possible when the wicked forsakes his way and returns to the Lord who abundantly pardons.
